Abstract

Abstract#

Sea ice forms from seawater at the interface between the ocean and the atmosphere. Its formation plays a key role for vertical exchange of salt and heat within the upper ocean and for the global thermohaline circulation, and its melt influences near-surface stratification of the polar and surrounding seas. The presence or absence of sea ice has large implications for human-related activities in the polar regions (especially the Arctic), including shipping, resource exploitation (incl. fishing) and the lifestyle of indigeneous communities.

The area fraction of sea ice is also commonly named the Sea Ice Concentration (SIC). It is a unitless number (the ratio of two areas) and is reported either as a number in the range [0;1] or as a percentage in the range [0-100%]. SIC is by far the most important sea ice variable when it comes to weather, ocean, and forecasting in the polar regions, and virtually all operational centers assimilate satellite-based products of SIC to guide there forecasts. Polar SIC is a key requirement for the Copernicus Imaging Microwave Radiometer (CIMR) mission. Timely information about SIC is also critical for safe navigation in ice-infested areas, which is why a dedicated SIC1H Level-2 algorithm was introduced for the CIMR mission.

Sea Ice Edge (SIED) is a binary information derived from SIC to quickly indicate where there is sea ice and where the ocean is free for ice. It is typically derived from a simple binary test involving a SIC threshold, classically 15% SIC (all regions with SIC larger than the threshold are considered ice-covered). From the binary product, one can derive the contour polygon of the ice cover. A dedicated SIED1H is also part of the CIMR product portofolio.

High latitude (and altitude) lakes will also freeze in winter. Lake Ice Cover is an important component of the cryosphere and changes to the timing of ice freeze-up, break-up, and duration can have substantial impacts on weather and climate, transportation, and ecology. While many lakes are smaller than the resolution CIMR can resolve, several large Northern Hemisphere inland water bodies that are critical to e.g. numerical weather prediction will be observed at least daily by the mission, with the potential of providing a high-revisit LIC information in between the less frequent high-resolution monitoring from optical or radar imagery.
